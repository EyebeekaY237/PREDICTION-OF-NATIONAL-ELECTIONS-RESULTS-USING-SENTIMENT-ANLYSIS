The Nigerian Coat of Arms

The official symbol of Nigeria's national identity, unity, and sovereignty

Official Nigerian Coat of Arms

Description

The Nigerian Coat of Arms is a heraldic emblem that represents the Federal Republic of Nigeria. It was officially adopted on May 20, 1960, just before Nigeria gained independence from Britain on October 1, 1960.

The black shield represents Nigeria's fertile soil, while the two white wavy bands on the shield symbolize the Niger and Benue Rivers that merge together in the country. The white horses on either side represent dignity, while the eagle stands for strength.

The green and white wreath at the base represents Nigeria's agricultural wealth, and the yellow flowers at the base (Costus spectabilis) are Nigeria's national flower.

Symbolic Elements and Their Meanings

The Black Shield

Represents Nigeria's fertile soil and the nation's agricultural heritage. The color black also symbolizes the strength and resilience of the Nigerian people.

Wavy White Bands

Depict the two major rivers of Nigeria - the Niger and Benue - which converge at Lokoja to form the distinctive "Y" shape seen on the shield.

Supporting Horses

The two white horses represent dignity and pride. They stand on either side of the shield as protectors of the nation's unity and sovereignty.

The Red Eagle

Symbolizes strength and the pride of Nigeria. The eagle stands atop the shield, watching over the nation and ready to defend its sovereignty.

The Ground

The green and white base represents Nigeria's rich agricultural land. The green stands for agriculture, while the white represents peace.

Costus Spectabilis

Nigeria's national flower decorates the base. Its yellow color represents the beauty and prosperity of the nation.

Historical Context

The Nigerian Coat of Arms was designed in 1960 by a British heraldry expert at the College of Arms in London, commissioned by the Nigerian government as part of preparations for independence.

It incorporates elements that reflect Nigeria's geography, natural resources, and aspirations as a new nation. The design was carefully chosen to represent unity in diversity, with the merging rivers symbolizing the coming together of Nigeria's many ethnic groups.

Since its adoption, the Coat of Arms has remained unchanged as a symbol of national continuity, appearing on government documents, official buildings, currency, and passports.

Evolution of Nigeria's National Symbols

1914

Amalgamation of Northern and Southern Protectorates under British rule. No official Nigerian emblem existed during colonial times.

1959

Design process begins for national symbols ahead of independence. A committee was formed to oversee the creation of national identity symbols.

1960

Official adoption of the Coat of Arms on May 20, 1960. First appeared on official documents at independence on October 1, 1960.

1963

Introduction of the Nigerian flag and Coat of Arms on the first series of the Nigerian pound banknotes.

1975

Standardization of the Coat of Arms design across all government uses to ensure consistency in representation.

2008

Official guidelines published for proper display and usage of the Coat of Arms to prevent misuse and maintain dignity.
